Understanding Your Website Tracking

Last updated June 2, 2026

This guide shows you what we can learn from your website, in plain language. No technical background needed. It covers who visits, what they do while they are there, and where to find the numbers yourself.

Everything here counts actions, never people by name. Think of it as a tally of helpful moments.

A snapshot of the last month

Here is what your website did in the 28 days up to June 1, 2026. Your live reports always show the latest, so treat this as a picture in time.

The basics
People who visitedabout 7,300
Visits in totalabout 9,000
Pages they looked atabout 13,800

Almost everyone was visiting for the first time, which means your website keeps reaching new people.

Where they came from. Roughly half of your visitors found you through a Google search, and roughly half came straight to your site by typing it in or following a saved link. A smaller number arrived from other websites and social media. Paid ads are not part of the picture yet, so this traffic is earned, not bought.

The languages they read in. Your audience is genuinely international. English is the most common, but after that your biggest groups read the site in Korean, Portuguese, Japanese, and French, with German and Arabic close behind. For a language school, that reach is a real asset.

What caught their interest. Your blog is doing a lot of work. The articles about English grammar and language facts each brought in hundreds of readers. Beyond the blog, the most visited pages were your home page, the San Diego school page, the booking page, and the General English course. When people browsed your courses and rooms, the San Diego shared apartments drew the most interest, followed by General English.

What they did. People clicked Apply 60 times, and your contact and sign-up forms were submitted 24 times. These are the moments that turn a visitor into an enquiry, so they are the numbers worth watching week to week.

Where to find your numbers

You don't need to set anything up. We've already built ready-made views in your Google Analytics:

  • CTA performance shows which buttons people click, where on the page, and in which language. Apply, Contact, and WhatsApp, side by side.
  • Behaviour & Conversions is the big picture: how many people visit, what they engage with, and how many take a key action.

To open them, click Explore in the left menu of Google Analytics and choose the view by name. To watch visitors in real time, open Reports, then Realtime. Every number can be filtered by language, so you can see how each audience behaves.

The actions worth watching

A few moments matter more than the rest, because they move someone closer to becoming a student. We mark the key ones in your Analytics as Key Events, which is Google's name for a conversion, so they stand out.

  • Apply. Someone clicked an Apply button. Your strongest sign of interest.
  • Contact. Someone clicked a Contact button to reach you.
  • WhatsApp. Someone clicked to message you on WhatsApp.
  • Form sent. Someone submitted a contact form, signed up to the newsletter, or booked a call.

A quick glossary, only if you're curious

If you ever open Analytics and see a word you don't recognize, here is the plain meaning.

What you seeWhat it means
apply_click, contact_click, whatsapp_clickSomeone clicked your Apply, Contact, or WhatsApp button
generate_leadSomeone sent a form or booked a call
select_item, view_itemSomeone clicked, then opened, a course or a room to learn more
select_contentSomeone clicked through to one of your blog articles
scroll_depth, faq_toggleHow far someone read, and which questions they opened

Google also records the basics on its own: page views, visits, new visitors, scrolling, file downloads, and site searches. You do not need to do anything for those.

What we do not track

Your visitors' privacy is protected by design. We never store anyone's name, we never record payment details, and email addresses and phone numbers are hidden before they reach the reports. We also follow your cookie banner. If someone declines, Google still counts the visit in a private way that never identifies the person.
